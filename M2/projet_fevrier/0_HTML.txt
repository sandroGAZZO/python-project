9 April 1821 – 31 August 1867) was a <a href="/wiki/French_poetry" title="French poetry">French poet</a> who also produced notable work as an essayist, art critic, and one of the first translators of <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Allan_Poe" title="Edgar Allan Poe">Edgar Allan Poe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>His most famous work, a book of <a href="/wiki/Lyric_poetry" title="Lyric poetry">lyric poetry</a> titled <i><a href="/wiki/Les_Fleurs_du_mal" title="Les Fleurs du mal">Les Fleurs du mal</a></i> (<i>The Flowers of Evil</i>), expresses the changing nature of beauty in the rapidly industrializing Paris during the mid-19th century. Baudelaire's highly original style of prose-poetry influenced a whole generation of poets including <a href="/wiki/Paul_Verlaine" title="Paul Verlaine">Paul Verlaine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Rimbaud" title="Arthur Rimbaud">Arthur Rimbaud</a> and <a href="/wiki/St%C3%A9phane_Mallarm%C3%A9" title="Stéphane Mallarmé">Stéphane Mallarmé</a>, among many others. He is credited with coining the term "<a href="/wiki/Modernity" title="Modernity">modernity</a>" (<i>modernité</i>) to designate the fleeting, ephemeral experience of life in an urban metropolis, and the responsibility of artistic expression to capture that experience.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Baudelaire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Baudelaire was born in Paris, France, on 9 April 1821, and baptized two months later at <a href="/wiki/Saint-Sulpice,_Paris" title="Saint-Sulpice, Paris">Saint-Sulpice</a> Roman Catholic Church.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> His father, Joseph-François Baudelaire (1759-1827),<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> a senior civil servant and amateur artist, was 34 years older than Baudelaire's mother, Caroline (née Dufaÿs) (1794-1871).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Joseph-François died during Baudelaire's childhood, at rue Hautefeuille, Paris, on February 10, 1827. The following year, Caroline married Lieutenant Colonel Jacques Aupick, who later became a French ambassador to various noble courts. Baudelaire's biographers have often seen this as a crucial moment, considering that finding himself no longer the sole focus of his mother's affection left him with a trauma, which goes some way to explaining the excesses later apparent in his life. He stated in a letter to her that, "There was in my childhood a period of passionate love for you."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Baudelaire regularly begged his mother for money throughout his career, often promising that a lucrative publishing contract or journalistic commission was just around the corner.
</p><p>Baudelaire was educated in <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">Lyon</a>, where he boarded. At 14, he was described by a classmate as "much more refined and distinguished than any of our fellow pupils...we are bound to one another...by shared tastes and sympathies, the precocious love of fine works of literature."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Baudelaire was erratic in his studies, at times diligent, at other times prone to "idleness". Later, he attended the <a href="/wiki/Lyc%C3%A9e_Louis-le-Grand" title="Lycée Louis-le-Grand">Lycée Louis-le-Grand</a> in Paris, studying law, a popular course for those not yet decided on any particular career. He began to frequent prostitutes and may have contracted gonorrhea and syphilis during this period. He also began to run up debts, mostly for clothes. Upon gaining his degree in 1839, he told his brother "I don't feel I have a vocation for anything." His stepfather had in mind a career in law or diplomacy, but instead Baudelaire decided to embark upon a literary career. His mother later recalled: "Oh, what grief! If Charles had let himself be guided by his stepfather, his career would have been very different...He would not have left a name in literature, it is true, but we should have been happier, all three of us."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Baudelaire_1844.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Baudelaire_1844.jpg/170px-Baudelaire_1844.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Baudelaire_1844.jpg/255px-Baudelaire_1844.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Baudelaire_1844.jpg/340px-Baudelaire_1844.jpg 2x" data-file-width="458" data-file-height="540" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Baudelaire_1844.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Portrait of Baudelaire, painted in 1844 by Emile Deroy (1820–1846)</div></div></div>
<p>His stepfather sent him on a voyage to Calcutta, India in 1841 in the hope of ending his dissolute habits. The trip provided strong impressions of the sea, sailing, and exotic ports, that he later employed in his poetry.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> (Baudelaire later exaggerated his aborted trip to create a legend about his youthful travels and experiences, including "riding on elephants".) On returning to the taverns of Paris, he began to compose some of the poems of "Les Fleurs du Mal". At 21, he received a sizable inheritance but squandered much of it within a few years. His family obtained a decree to place his property in trust,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> which he resented bitterly, at one point arguing that allowing him to fail financially would have been the one sure way of teaching him to keep his finances in order.
</p><p>Baudelaire became known in artistic circles as a <a href="/wiki/Dandy" title="Dandy">dandy</a> and free-spender, going through much of his inheritance and allowance in a short period of time. During this time, <a href="/wiki/Jeanne_Duval" title="Jeanne Duval">Jeanne Duval</a> became his mistress. She was rejected by his family. His mother thought Duval a "Black Venus" who "tortured him in every way" and drained him of money at every opportunity.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Baudelaire made a suicide attempt during this period.
</p><p>He took part in the <a href="/wiki/Revolutions_of_1848_in_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Revolutions of 1848 in France">Revolutions of 1848</a> and wrote for a revolutionary newspaper. However, his interest in politics was passing, as he was later to note in his journals.
</p><p>In the early 1850s, Baudelaire struggled with poor health, pressing debts, and irregular literary output. He often moved from one lodging to another to escape creditors. He undertook many projects that he was unable to complete, though he did finish translations of stories by <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Allan_Poe" title="Edgar Allan Poe">Edgar Allan Poe</a>.
</p><p>Upon the death of his stepfather in 1857, Baudelaire received no mention in the will but he was heartened nonetheless that the division with his mother might now be mended. At 36, he wrote her: "believe that I belong to you absolutely, and that I belong only to you."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> His mother died on 16 August 1871, outliving her son by almost four years.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Published_career">Published career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Baudelaire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Published career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>His first published work, under the pseudonym Baudelaire Dufaÿs,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> was his art review "Salon of 1845", which attracted immediate attention for its boldness. Many of his critical opinions were novel in their time, including his championing of <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix" title="Eugène Delacroix">Delacroix</a>, and some of his views seem remarkably in tune with the future theories of the Impressionist painters.
</p><p>In 1846, Baudelaire wrote his second Salon review, gaining additional credibility as an advocate and critic of <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romanticism</a>. His continued support of <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix" title="Eugène Delacroix">Delacroix</a> as the foremost Romantic artist gained widespread notice.<sup id="cite_ref-crapkq_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crapkq-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The following year Baudelaire's novella <i><a href="/wiki/La_Fanfarlo" title="La Fanfarlo">La Fanfarlo</a></i> was published.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Flowers_of_Evil"><i>The Flowers of Evil</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Baudelaire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Flowers of Evil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Fleurs_du_mal.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Fleurs_du_mal.jpg/170px-Fleurs_du_mal.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="282" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Fleurs_du_mal.jpg/255px-Fleurs_du_mal.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Fleurs_du_mal.jpg/340px-Fleurs_du_mal.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1923" data-file-height="3187" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Fleurs_du_mal.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The first edition of <i><a href="/wiki/Les_Fleurs_du_mal" title="Les Fleurs du mal">Les Fleurs du mal</a></i> with author's notes</div></div></div>
<p>Baudelaire was a slow and very attentive worker. However, he often was sidetracked by indolence, emotional distress and illness, and it was not until 1857 that he published <i><a href="/wiki/Les_Fleurs_du_mal" title="Les Fleurs du mal">Les Fleurs du mal</a></i> (<i>The Flowers of Evil</i>), his first and most famous volume of poems.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Some of these poems had already appeared in the <i><a href="/wiki/Revue_des_deux_mondes" class="mw-redirect" title="Revue des deux mondes">Revue des deux mondes</a></i> (<i>Review of Two Worlds</i>) in 1855, when they were published by Baudelaire's friend <a href="/wiki/Auguste_Poulet_Malassis" class="mw-redirect" title="Auguste Poulet Malassis">Auguste Poulet Malassis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Some of the poems had appeared as "fugitive verse" in various French magazines during the previous decade.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The poems found a small, yet appreciative audience. However, greater public attention was given to their subject matter. The effect on fellow artists was, as <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9odore_de_Banville" title="Théodore de Banville">Théodore de Banville</a> stated, "immense, prodigious, unexpected, mingled with admiration and with some indefinable anxious fear".<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Flaubert" title="Gustave Flaubert">Gustave Flaubert</a>, recently attacked in a similar fashion for <i><a href="/wiki/Madame_Bovary" title="Madame Bovary">Madame Bovary</a></i> (and acquitted), was impressed and wrote to Baudelaire: "You have found a way to rejuvenate Romanticism...You are as unyielding as marble, and as penetrating as an English mist."<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The principal themes of sex and death were considered scandalous for the period. He also touched on lesbianism, sacred and profane love, metamorphosis, melancholy, the corruption of the city, lost innocence, the oppressiveness of living, and wine. Notable in some poems is Baudelaire's use of imagery of the sense of smell and of fragrances, which is used to evoke feelings of nostalgia and past intimacy.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The book, however, quickly became a byword for unwholesomeness among mainstream critics of the day. Some critics called a few of the poems "masterpieces of passion, art and poetry," but other poems were deemed to merit no less than legal action to suppress them.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> J. Habas led the charge against Baudelaire, writing in <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Figaro" title="Le Figaro">Le Figaro</a></i>: "Everything in it which is not hideous is incomprehensible, everything one understands is putrid." Baudelaire responded to the outcry in a prophetic letter to his mother:
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"You know that I have always considered that literature and the arts pursue an aim independent of morality. Beauty of conception and style is enough for me. But this book, whose title (<i>Fleurs du mal</i>) says everything, is clad, as you will see, in a cold and sinister beauty. It was created with rage and patience. Besides, the proof of its positive worth is in all the ill that they speak of it. The book enrages people. Moreover, since I was terrified myself of the horror that I should inspire, I cut out a third from the proofs. They deny me everything, the spirit of invention and even the knowledge of the French language. I don't care a rap about all these imbeciles, and I know that this book, with its virtues and its faults, will make its way in the memory of the lettered public, beside the best poems of V. Hugo, Th. Gautier and even Byron."<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rops_Les_Epaves_1866.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Rops_Les_Epaves_1866.jpg/170px-Rops_Les_Epaves_1866.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="267" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Rops_Les_Epaves_1866.jpg/255px-Rops_Les_Epaves_1866.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Rops_Les_Epaves_1866.jpg/340px-Rops_Les_Epaves_1866.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1127" data-file-height="1772" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rops_Les_Epaves_1866.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illustration cover for <i>Les Épaves</i>, by Baudelaire's friend <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9licien_Rops" title="Félicien Rops">Félicien Rops</a></div></div></div>
<p>Baudelaire, his publisher and the printer successfully were prosecuted for creating an offense against public morals. They were fined, but Baudelaire was not imprisoned.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Six of the poems were suppressed, but printed later as <i>Les Épaves</i> (<i>The Wrecks</i>) (Brussels, 1866). Another edition of <i>Les Fleurs du mal</i>, without these poems, but with considerable additions, appeared in 1861. Many notables rallied behind Baudelaire and condemned the sentence. <a href="/wiki/Victor_Hugo" title="Victor Hugo">Victor Hugo</a> wrote to him: "Your <i>fleurs du mal</i> shine and dazzle like stars...I applaud your vigorous spirit with all my might."<sup id="cite_ref-yvjown_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yvjown-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Baudelaire did not appeal the judgment, but his fine was reduced. Nearly 100 years later, on May 11, 1949, Baudelaire was vindicated, the judgment officially reversed, and the six banned poems reinstated in France.<sup id="cite_ref-yvjown_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yvjown-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the poem "Au lecteur" ("To the Reader") that prefaces <i>Les Fleurs du mal</i>, Baudelaire accuses his readers of hypocrisy and of being as guilty of sins and lies as the poet:
</p>
<dl><dd>... If rape or arson, poison or the knife</dd>
<dd>Has wove no pleasing patterns in the stuff</dd>
<dd>Of this drab canvas we accept as life—</dd>
<dd>It is because we are not bold enough!
<dl><dd>(<a href="/wiki/Roy_Campbell_(poet)" title="Roy Campbell (poet)">Roy Campbell</a>'s translation)</dd></dl></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_years">Final years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Baudelaire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Final years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Charles_Baudelaire.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Charles_Baudelaire.jpg/220px-Charles_Baudelaire.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="324" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Charles_Baudelaire.jpg/330px-Charles_Baudelaire.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Charles_Baudelaire.jpg/440px-Charles_Baudelaire.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="2941" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Charles_Baudelaire.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Charles Baudelaire by <a href="/wiki/Nadar_(photographer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nadar (photographer)">Nadar</a>, 1855</div></div></div>
<p>Baudelaire next worked on a translation and adaptation of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_De_Quincey" title="Thomas De Quincey">Thomas De Quincey</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Confessions_of_an_English_Opium_Eater" class="mw-redirect" title="Confessions of an English Opium Eater">Confessions of an English Opium Eater</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Other works in the years that followed included <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Spleen_de_Paris" title="Le Spleen de Paris">Petits Poèmes en prose</a></i> (<i>Small <a href="/wiki/Prose_poetry" title="Prose poetry">Prose poems</a></i>); a series of art reviews published in the <i>Pays, Exposition universelle</i> (<i>Country, World Fair</i>); studies on <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Flaubert" title="Gustave Flaubert">Gustave Flaubert</a> (in <i><a href="/wiki/L%27Artiste" title="L&#39;Artiste">L'Artiste</a></i>, October 18, 1857); on <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9ophile_Gautier" title="Théophile Gautier">Théophile Gautier</a> (<i>Revue contemporaine</i>, September 1858); various articles contributed to Eugene Crepet's <i>Poètes francais</i>; <i><a href="/wiki/Les_Paradis_artificiels" title="Les Paradis artificiels">Les Paradis artificiels</a>: opium et haschisch</i> (<i>French poets; Artificial Paradises: opium and hashish</i>) (1860); and <i>Un Dernier Chapitre de l'histoire des oeuvres de Balzac</i> (<i>A Final Chapter of the history of works of Balzac</i>) (1880), originally an article "Comment on paye ses dettes quand on a du génie" ("How one pays one's debts when one has genius"), in which his criticism turns against his friends <a href="/wiki/Honor%C3%A9_de_Balzac" title="Honoré de Balzac">Honoré de Balzac</a>, <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9ophile_Gautier" title="Théophile Gautier">Théophile Gautier</a>, and <a href="/wiki/G%C3%A9rard_de_Nerval" title="Gérard de Nerval">Gérard de Nerval</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>By 1859, his illnesses, his long-term use of <a href="/wiki/Laudanum" title="Laudanum">laudanum</a>, his life of stress, and his poverty had taken a toll and Baudelaire had aged noticeably. But at last, his mother relented and agreed to let him live with her for a while at <a href="/wiki/Honfleur" title="Honfleur">Honfleur</a>. Baudelaire was productive and at peace in the seaside town, his poem <i>Le Voyage</i> being one example of his efforts during that time.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> In 1860, he became an ardent supporter of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a>.
</p><p>His financial difficulties increased again, however, particularly after his publisher Poulet Malassis went bankrupt in 1861. In 1864, he left Paris for Belgium, partly in the hope of selling the rights to his works and to give lectures.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> His long-standing relationship with <a href="/wiki/Jeanne_Duval" title="Jeanne Duval">Jeanne Duval</a> continued on-and-off, and he helped her to the end of his life. Baudelaire's relationships with actress Marie Daubrun and with courtesan <a href="/wiki/Apollonie_Sabatier" title="Apollonie Sabatier">Apollonie Sabatier</a>, though the source of much inspiration, never produced any lasting satisfaction. He smoked <a href="/wiki/Opium" title="Opium">opium</a>, and in Brussels he began to drink to excess. Baudelaire suffered a massive stroke in 1866 and paralysis followed. After more than a year of <a href="/wiki/Aphasia" title="Aphasia">aphasia</a>, he received the <a href="/wiki/Last_rites" title="Last rites">last rites</a> of the Catholic Church.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The last two years of his life were spent in a semi-paralyzed state in "maisons de santé" in Brussels and in Paris, where he died on 31 August 1867. Baudelaire is buried in the <a href="/wiki/Cimeti%C3%A8re_du_Montparnasse" class="mw-redirect" title="Cimetière du Montparnasse">Cimetière du Montparnasse</a>, Paris.
</p><p>Many of Baudelaire's works were published posthumously. After his death, his mother paid off his substantial debts, and she found some comfort in Baudelaire's emerging fame. "I see that my son, for all his faults, has his place in literature." She lived another four years.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Poetry">Poetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Baudelaire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Poetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r960796168">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Who among us has not dreamt, in moments of ambition, of the miracle of a poetic prose, musical without rhythm and rhyme, supple and staccato enough to adapt to the lyrical stirrings of the soul, the undulations of dreams, and sudden leaps of consciousness. This obsessive idea is above all a child of giant cities, of the intersecting of their myriad relations.</p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8201;<cite>Dedication of <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Spleen_de_Paris" title="Le Spleen de Paris">Le Spleen de Paris</a></i></cite></div></blockquote>
<p>Baudelaire is one of the major innovators in French literature. His poetry is influenced by the French romantic poets of the earlier 19th century, although its attention to the formal features of verse connects it more closely to the work of the contemporary "Parnassians". As for theme and tone, in his works we see the rejection of the belief in the supremacy of nature and the fundamental goodness of man as typically espoused by the romantics and expressed by them in rhetorical, effusive and public voice in favor of a new urban sensibility, an awareness of individual moral complexity, an interest in vice (linked with <a href="/wiki/Decadent_movement" title="Decadent movement">decadence</a>) and refined sensual and aesthetic pleasures, and the use of urban subject matter, such as the city, the crowd, individual passers-by, all expressed in highly ordered verse, sometimes through a cynical and ironic voice. Formally, the use of sound to create atmosphere, and of "symbols" (images that take on an expanded function within the poem), betray a move towards considering the poem as a self-referential object, an idea further developed by the Symbolists <a href="/wiki/Paul_Verlaine" title="Paul Verlaine">Verlaine</a> and <a href="/wiki/St%C3%A9phane_Mallarm%C3%A9" title="Stéphane Mallarmé">Mallarmé</a>, who acknowledge Baudelaire as a pioneer in this regard.
</p><p>Beyond his innovations in versification and the theories of symbolism and "correspondences", an awareness of which is essential to any appreciation of the literary value of his work, aspects of his work that regularly receive much critical discussion include the role of women, the theological direction of his work and his alleged advocacy of "satanism", his experience of drug-induced states of mind, the figure of the dandy, his stance regarding democracy and its implications for the individual, his response to the spiritual uncertainties of the time, his criticisms of the bourgeois, and his advocacy of modern music and painting (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Wagner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix" title="Eugène Delacroix">Delacroix</a>). He made Paris the subject of modern poetry. He brought the city's details to life in the eyes and hearts of his readers.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critiques">Critiques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Baudelaire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Critiques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Baudelaire was an active participant in the artistic life of his times. As critic and essayist, he wrote extensively and perceptively about the luminaries and themes of French culture. He was frank with friends and enemies, rarely took the diplomatic approach and sometimes responded violently verbally, that often undermined his cause.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> His associations were numerous, including <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Courbet" title="Gustave Courbet">Gustave Courbet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honor%C3%A9_Daumier" title="Honoré Daumier">Honoré Daumier</a>, <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9licien_Rops" title="Félicien Rops">Félicien Rops</a>, <a href="/wiki/Franz_Liszt" title="Franz Liszt">Franz Liszt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Champfleury" title="Champfleury">Champfleury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Victor_Hugo" title="Victor Hugo">Victor Hugo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Flaubert" title="Gustave Flaubert">Gustave Flaubert</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Balzac" class="mw-redirect" title="Balzac">Balzac</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Edgar_Allan_Poe">Edgar Allan Poe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Baudelaire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Edgar Allan Poe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1847, Baudelaire became acquainted with the works of <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Allan_Poe" title="Edgar Allan Poe">Poe</a>, in which he found tales and poems that had, he claimed, long existed in his own brain but never taken shape. Baudelaire saw in Poe a precursor and tried to be his French contemporary counterpart.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> From this time until 1865, he was largely occupied with translating Poe's works; his translations were widely praised. Baudelaire was not the first French translator of Poe, but his "scrupulous translations" were considered among the best. These were published as <i>Histoires extraordinaires</i> (<i>Extraordinary stories</i>) (1856), <i>Nouvelles histoires extraordinaires</i> (<i>New extraordinary stories</i>) (1857), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Narrative_of_Arthur_Gordon_Pym_of_Nantucket" title="The Narrative of Arthur Gordon Pym of Nantucket">Aventures d'Arthur Gordon Pym</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eureka:_A_Prose_Poem" title="Eureka: A Prose Poem">Eureka</a></i>, and <i>Histoires grotesques et sérieuses</i> (<i>Grotesque and serious stories</i>) (1865). Two essays on Poe are to be found in his <i>Œuvres complètes</i> (<i>Complete works</i>) (vols. v. and vi.).
</p>
<h3><span id="Eug.C3.A8ne_Delacroix"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Eugène_Delacroix">Eugène Delacroix</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Baudelaire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Eugène Delacroix">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A strong supporter of the Romantic painter <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix" title="Eugène Delacroix">Delacroix</a>, Baudelaire called him "a poet in painting". Baudelaire also absorbed much of Delacroix's aesthetic ideas as expressed in his journals. As Baudelaire elaborated in his "Salon of 1846", "As one contemplates his series of pictures, one seems to be attending the celebration of some grievous mystery...This grave and lofty melancholy shines with a dull light.. plaintive and profound like a melody by Weber."<sup id="cite_ref-crapkq_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crapkq-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Delacroix, though appreciative, kept his distance from Baudelaire, particularly after the scandal of <i>Les Fleurs du mal</i>. In private correspondence, Delacroix stated that Baudelaire "really gets on my nerves" and he expressed his unhappiness with Baudelaire's persistent comments about "melancholy" and "feverishness".<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Richard_Wagner">Richard Wagner</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Baudelaire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Richard Wagner">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Baudelaire had no formal musical training, and knew little of composers beyond <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Beethoven</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carl_Maria_von_Weber" title="Carl Maria von Weber">Weber</a>. Weber was in some ways <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Wagner</a>'s precursor, using the <a href="/wiki/Leitmotif" title="Leitmotif">leitmotif</a> and conceiving the idea of the "total art work" ("Gesamtkunstwerk"), both of which gained Baudelaire's admiration. Before even hearing Wagner's music, Baudelaire studied reviews and essays about him, and formulated his impressions. Later, Baudelaire put them into his non-technical analysis of Wagner, which was highly regarded, particularly his essay "Richard Wagner et Tannhäuser à Paris".<sup id="cite_ref-yhkyza_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yhkyza-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Baudelaire's reaction to music was passionate and psychological. "Music engulfs (possesses) me like the sea."<sup id="cite_ref-yhkyza_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yhkyza-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> After attending three Wagner concerts in Paris in 1860, Baudelaire wrote to the composer: "I had a feeling of pride and joy in understanding, in being possessed, in being overwhelmed, a truly sensual pleasure like that of rising in the air."<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Baudelaire's writings contributed to the elevation of Wagner and to the cult of <a href="/wiki/Wagner_controversies" title="Wagner controversies">Wagnerism</a> that swept Europe in the following decades.
</p>
<h3><span id="Th.C3.A9ophile_Gautier"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Théophile_Gautier">Théophile Gautier</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Baudelaire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Théophile Gautier">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9ophile_Gautier" title="Théophile Gautier">Gautier</a>, writer and poet, earned Baudelaire's respect for his perfection of form and his mastery of language, though Baudelaire thought he lacked deeper emotion and spirituality. Both strove to express the artist's inner vision, which <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Heine" title="Heinrich Heine">Heinrich Heine</a> earlier stated: "In artistic matters, I am a supernaturalist. I believe that the artist can not find all his forms in nature, but that the most remarkable are revealed to him in his soul."<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Gautier's frequent meditations on death and the horror of life are themes which influenced Baudelaire's writings. In gratitude for their friendship and commonality of vision, Baudelaire dedicated <i>Les Fleurs du mal</i> to Gautier.
</p>
<h3><span id=".C3.89douard_Manet"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Édouard_Manet">Édouard Manet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Baudelaire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Édouard Manet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Edouard_Manet_-_Manet-98302_-_Charles_Baudelaire_(1865).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Edouard_Manet_-_Manet-98302_-_Charles_Baudelaire_%281865%29.jpg/220px-Edouard_Manet_-_Manet-98302_-_Charles_Baudelaire_%281865%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="246" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Edouard_Manet_-_Manet-98302_-_Charles_Baudelaire_%281865%29.jpg/330px-Edouard_Manet_-_Manet-98302_-_Charles_Baudelaire_%281865%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Edouard_Manet_-_Manet-98302_-_Charles_Baudelaire_%281865%29.jpg/440px-Edouard_Manet_-_Manet-98302_-_Charles_Baudelaire_%281865%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1741" data-file-height="1950" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Edouard_Manet_-_Manet-98302_-_Charles_Baudelaire_(1865).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i lang="fr" title="French language text">Charles Baudelaire, de face</i> (1869 print of 1865 etching) by <a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard_Manet" title="Édouard Manet">Édouard Manet</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard_Manet" title="Édouard Manet">Manet</a> and Baudelaire became constant companions from around 1855. In the early 1860s, Baudelaire accompanied Manet on daily sketching trips and often met him socially. Manet also lent Baudelaire money and looked after his affairs, particularly when Baudelaire went to Belgium. Baudelaire encouraged Manet to strike out on his own path and not succumb to criticism. "Manet has great talent, a talent which will stand the test of time. But he has a weak character. He seems to me crushed and stunned by shock."<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> In his painting <i><a href="/wiki/Music_in_the_Tuileries" title="Music in the Tuileries">Music in the Tuileries</a></i>, Manet includes portraits of his friends <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9ophile_Gautier" title="Théophile Gautier">Théophile Gautier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Offenbach" title="Jacques Offenbach">Jacques Offenbach</a>, and Baudelaire.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> While it's difficult to differentiate who influenced whom, both Manet and Baudelaire discussed and expressed some common themes through their respective arts. Baudelaire praised the modernity of Manet's subject matter: "almost all our originality comes from the stamp that 'time' imprints upon our feelings."<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> When Manet's famous <i><a href="/wiki/Olympia_(painting)" class="mw-redirect" title="Olympia (painting)">Olympia</a></i> (1863), a portrait of a nude prostitute, provoked a scandal for its blatant realism mixed with an imitation of <a href="/wiki/Sleeping_Venus_(Giorgione)" title="Sleeping Venus (Giorgione)">Renaissance motifs</a>, Baudelaire worked privately to support his friend, though he offered no public defense (he was, however, ill at the time). When Baudelaire returned from Belgium after his stroke, Manet and his wife were frequent visitors at the nursing home and she played passages from Wagner for Baudelaire on the piano.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nadar">Nadar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Baudelaire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Nadar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nadar_(photographer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nadar (photographer)">Nadar</a> (Félix Tournachon) was a noted caricaturist, scientist and important early photographer. Baudelaire admired Nadar, one of his close friends, and wrote: "Nadar is the most amazing manifestation of vitality."<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> They moved in similar circles and Baudelaire made many social connections through him. Nadar's ex-mistress <a href="/wiki/Jeanne_Duval" title="Jeanne Duval">Jeanne Duval</a> became Baudelaire's mistress around 1842. Baudelaire became interested in photography in the 1850s, and denouncing it as an art form, advocated its return to "its real purpose, which is that of being the servant to the sciences and arts". Photography should not, according to Baudelaire, encroach upon "the domain of the impalpable and the imaginary".<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Nadar remained a stalwart friend right to Baudelaire's last days and wrote his obituary notice in <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Figaro" title="Le Figaro">Le Figaro</a></i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy">Philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Baudelaire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many of Baudelaire's philosophical proclamations were considered scandalous and intentionally provocative in his time. He wrote on a wide range of subjects, drawing criticism and outrage from many quarters.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Baudelaire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gustave_Courbet_033.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Gustave_Courbet_033.jpg/220px-Gustave_Courbet_033.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="183" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Gustave_Courbet_033.jpg/330px-Gustave_Courbet_033.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Gustave_Courbet_033.jpg/440px-Gustave_Courbet_033.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1649" data-file-height="1368" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gustave_Courbet_033.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Portrait by <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Courbet" title="Gustave Courbet">Gustave Courbet</a>, 1848</div></div></div>
<p>Baudelaire's influence on the direction of modern French (and English) language literature was considerable. The most significant French writers to come after him were generous with tributes; four years after his death, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Rimbaud" title="Arthur Rimbaud">Arthur Rimbaud</a> praised him in a letter as 'the king of poets, a true God'.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> In 1895, <a href="/wiki/St%C3%A9phane_Mallarm%C3%A9" title="Stéphane Mallarmé">Stéphane Mallarmé</a> published "Le Tombeau de Charles Baudelaire", a sonnet in Baudelaire's memory. <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Proust" title="Marcel Proust">Marcel Proust</a>, in an essay published in 1922, stated that along with <a href="/wiki/Alfred_de_Vigny" title="Alfred de Vigny">Alfred de Vigny</a>, Baudelaire was "the greatest poet of the nineteenth century".<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the English-speaking world, <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Wilson" title="Edmund Wilson">Edmund Wilson</a> credited Baudelaire as providing an initial impetus for the <a href="/wiki/Symbolism_(arts)" title="Symbolism (arts)">Symbolist</a> movement by virtue of his translations of Poe.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> In 1930, <a href="/wiki/T.S._Eliot" class="mw-redirect" title="T.S. Eliot">T.S. Eliot</a>, while asserting that Baudelaire had not yet received a "just appreciation" even in France, claimed that the poet had "great genius" and asserted that his "technical mastery which can hardly be overpraised...has made his verse an inexhaustible study for later poets, not only in his own language".<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> In a lecture delivered in French on "Edgar Allan Poe and France" (Edgar Poe et la France) in Aix-en-Provence in April 1948, Eliot stated that "I am an English poet of American origin who learnt his art under the aegis of Baudelaire and the Baudelairian lineage of poets."<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Eliot also alluded to Baudelaire's poetry directly in his own poetry. For example, he quoted the last line of Baudelaire's "Au Lecteur" in the last line of Section I of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Waste_Land" title="The Waste Land">The Waste Land</a></i>.'<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>At the same time that Eliot was affirming Baudelaire's importance from a broadly conservative and explicitly Christian viewpoint,<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Left-wing_politics" title="Left-wing politics">left-wing</a> critics such as Wilson and <a href="/wiki/Walter_Benjamin" title="Walter Benjamin">Walter Benjamin</a> were able to do so from a dramatically different perspective. Benjamin translated Baudelaire's <i>Tableaux Parisiens</i> into German and published a major essay on translation<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> as the foreword.
</p><p>In the late 1930s, Benjamin used Baudelaire as a starting point and focus for <i><a href="/wiki/Arcades_Project" title="Arcades Project">Das Passagenwerk</a></i>, his monumental attempt at a <a href="/wiki/Historical_materialism" title="Historical materialism">materialist</a> assessment of 19th-century culture.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> For Benjamin, Baudelaire's importance lay in his anatomies of the crowd, of the city and of modernity.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> He says that, in <i><a href="/wiki/Les_Fleurs_du_mal" title="Les Fleurs du mal">Les Fleurs du mal</a></i>, "the specific devaluation of the world of things, as manifested in the commodity, is the foundation of Baudelaire's allegorical intention."<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Porche" class="mw-redirect" title="François Porche">François Porche</a> published a poetry collection called <i>Charles Baudelaire: Poetry Collection</i> in memory of Baudelaire.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Vanderbilt University has "assembled one of the world's most comprehensive research collections on...Baudelaire".<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> <i>Les Fleurs du mal</i> has a number of scholarly references.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_adaptations">Musical adaptations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Baudelaire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Musical adaptations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>French composer <a href="/wiki/Claude_Debussy" title="Claude Debussy">Claude Debussy</a> set five of Baudelaire's poems to music in 1890: <i><a href="/wiki/Cinq_po%C3%A8mes_de_Charles_Baudelaire" title="Cinq poèmes de Charles Baudelaire">Cinq poèmes de Charles Baudelaire</a></i> (<i>Le Balcon</i>, <i>Harmonie du soir</i>, <i>Le Jet d'eau</i>, <i>Recueillement</i> and <i>La Mort des amants</i>).</li>
<li>French composer <a href="/wiki/Henri_Duparc_(composer)" title="Henri Duparc (composer)">Henri Duparc</a> set two of Beaudelaire's poems to music: "L'Invitation au voyage" in 1870, and "La vie antérieure" in 1884.</li>
<li>English composer <a href="/wiki/Mark-Anthony_Turnage" title="Mark-Anthony Turnage">Mark-Anthony Turnage</a> composed settings of two of Baudelaire's poems, "Harmonie du soir" and "L'Invitation au voyage", for soprano and seven instruments.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>American electronic musician <a href="/wiki/Ruth_White_(composer)" title="Ruth White (composer)">Ruth White (composer)</a> recorded some of Baudelaire's poems in <i><a href="/wiki/Les_Fleurs_du_Mal" class="mw-redirect" title="Les Fleurs du Mal">Les Fleurs du Mal</a></i> as chants over electronic music in a 1969 recording, <i>Flowers of Evil</i>.</li>
<li>French singer-songwriter <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9o_Ferr%C3%A9" title="Léo Ferré">Léo Ferré</a> devoted himself to set Baudelaire's poetry into music in three albums: <i><a href="/wiki/Les_Fleurs_du_mal_(L%C3%A9o_Ferr%C3%A9_album)" title="Les Fleurs du mal (Léo Ferré album)">Les Fleurs du mal</a></i> in 1957 (12 poems), <i><a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9o_Ferr%C3%A9_chante_Baudelaire" title="Léo Ferré chante Baudelaire">Léo Ferré chante Baudelaire</a></i> in 1967 (24 poems, including one from <i>Le Spleen de Paris</i>), and the posthumous <i><a href="/wiki/Les_Fleurs_du_mal_(suite_et_fin)" title="Les Fleurs du mal (suite et fin)">Les Fleurs du mal (suite et fin)</a></i> (21 poems), recorded in 1977 but released in 2008.</li>
<li>Soviet/Russian composer <a href="/wiki/David_Tukhmanov" title="David Tukhmanov">David Tukhmanov</a> has set Baudelaire's poem to music (cult album <i>On a Wave of My Memory</i>, 1975).<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>American avant-garde composer, vocalist and performer <a href="/wiki/Diamanda_Gal%C3%A1s" title="Diamanda Galás">Diamanda Galás</a> made an interpretation in original French of <i><a href="/wiki/Les_Litanies_de_Satan" title="Les Litanies de Satan">Les Litanies de Satan</a></i> from <i><a href="/wiki/Les_Fleurs_du_mal" title="Les Fleurs du mal">Les Fleurs du mal</a></i>, in her debut album titled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Litanies_of_Satan" title="The Litanies of Satan">The Litanies of Satan</a></i>, which consists of tape and electronics effects with layers of her voice.</li>
<li>French singer David TMX recorded the poems "Lesbos" and "Une Charogne" from <i>The Flowers of Evil</i>.</li>
<li>French metal/shoegaze groups <a href="/wiki/Alcest" title="Alcest">Alcest</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amesoeurs" title="Amesoeurs">Amesoeurs</a> used his poetry for the lyrics of the tracks "Élévation" (on <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Secret_(EP)" title="Le Secret (EP)">Le Secret</a></i>) and "Recueillement" (on <i><a href="/wiki/Amesoeurs_(album)" title="Amesoeurs (album)">Amesoeurs</a></i>), respectively. <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Frost" title="Celtic Frost">Celtic Frost</a> used his poem <i><a href="/wiki/Les_Fleurs_du_mal" title="Les Fleurs du mal">Tristesses de la lune</a></i> as a lyrics for song on album <i><a href="/wiki/Into_the_Pandemonium" title="Into the Pandemonium">Into the Pandemonium</a></i>.</li>
<li>French Black Metal bands Mortifera and <a href="/wiki/Peste_Noire" title="Peste Noire">Peste Noire</a> used Baudelaire's poems as lyrics for the songs "Le revenant" and "Ciel brouillé" (on <i>Vastiia Tenebrd Mortifera</i> by Mortifera) and "Le mort joyeux" and "Spleen" (on <i><a href="/wiki/La_Sanie_des_si%C3%A8cles_%E2%80%93_Pan%C3%A9gyrique_de_la_d%C3%A9g%C3%A9n%C3%A9rescence" title="La Sanie des siècles – Panégyrique de la dégénérescence">La Sanie des siècles – Panégyrique de la dégénérescence</a></i> by Peste Noire)</li>
<li>Israeli singer Maor Cohen's 2005 album, the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew" class="mw-redirect" title="Hebrew">Hebrew</a> name of which translates to French as "Les Fleurs du Mal", is a compilation of songs from Baudelaire's book of the same name. The texts were translated to Hebrew by Israeli poet Dori Manor, and the music was composed by Cohen.</li>
<li>Italian singer <a href="/wiki/Franco_Battiato" title="Franco Battiato">Franco Battiato</a> set <i>Invitation au voyage</i> to music as <i>Invito Al Viaggio</i> on his 1999 album <i>Fleurs (Esempi Affini Di Scritture E Simili)</i>.</li>
<li>American composer <a href="/wiki/G%C3%A9rard_Pape" title="Gérard Pape">Gérard Pape</a> set <i>Tristesses de la lune/Sorrows of the Moon</i> from <i>Fleurs du Mal</i> for voice and electronic tape.</li>
<li>French band Marc Seberg wrote an adaptation of Recueillement for their 1985 album <i>Le Chant Des Terres</i>.</li>
<li>Russian heavy metal band <a href="/wiki/Black_Obelisk_(band)" title="Black Obelisk (band)">Black Obelisk</a> used Russian translations of several Baudelaire poems as lyrics for their songs.</li>
<li>French singer <a href="/wiki/Myl%C3%A8ne_Farmer" title="Mylène Farmer">Mylène Farmer</a> performed "L'Horloge" to music by <a href="/wiki/Laurent_Boutonnat" title="Laurent Boutonnat">Laurent Boutonnat</a> on the album <i>Ainsi soi je</i> and the opening number of her 1989 concert tour. On her latest album "Désobéissance" (2018) she recorded Baudelaire's preface to "Les Fleurs du Mal", "Au lecteur". The French journalist Hugues Royer mentioned several allusions and interpretations of Baudelaire's poems and quotations used by Farmer in various songs in his book "Mylène" <sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> (published in 2008).</li>
<li>In 2009 the Italian rock band <a href="/wiki/C.F.F._e_il_Nomade_Venerabile" title="C.F.F. e il Nomade Venerabile">C.F.F. e il Nomade Venerabile</a> released <i>Un jour noir</i>, a song inspired by <i>Spleen</i>, contained in the album <i>Lucidinervi</i> (Otium Records / <a href="/wiki/Compagnia_Nuove_Indye" class="mw-redirect" title="Compagnia Nuove Indye">Compagnia Nuove Indye</a>). The video clip is available on YouTube.</li>
<li>German aggrotech band C-Drone-Defect used the translation of "Le Rebelle" by <a href="/wiki/Roy_Campbell_(poet)" title="Roy Campbell (poet)">Roy Campbell</a> as lyrics for the song "Rebellis" on their 2009 album <i>Dystopia</i>.</li>
<li>English rock band <a href="/wiki/The_Cure" title="The Cure">The Cure</a> used the translation of "Les yeux des pauvres" as lyrics for the song "How Beautiful You Are".</li>
<li>French singer-songwriter and musician <a href="/wiki/Serge_Gainsbourg" title="Serge Gainsbourg">Serge Gainsbourg</a> has set Baudelaire's poem "Dancing Snake" (Le serpent qui danse) to music in his 1964 song "Baudelaire".</li>
<li>Greek black metal band <a href="/wiki/Rotting_Christ" title="Rotting Christ">Rotting Christ</a> adapted Baudelaire's poem "Les Litanies De Satan" from <i>Fleurs du Mal</i> for a song of the same name in their 2016 album <i>Rituals</i>.</li>
<li>Belgian female-fronted band <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exsangue&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Exsangue (page does not exist)">Exsangue</a> released the debut video for the single "<a href="/w/index.php?title=A_une_Malabaraise&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="A une Malabaraise (page does not exist)">A une Malabaraise</a>", and the lyrics are based on Baudelaire's same-named sonnet in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Belgian electronic music band Modern Cubism has released two albums where poems of Baudelaire are used as lyrics, <i>Les Plaintes d’un Icare</i> in 2008, and live album <i>Live Complaints</i> in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
